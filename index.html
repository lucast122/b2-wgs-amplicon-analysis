Soil microbiome response to drought in the Biosphere 2 tropical rainforest

This analysis extends our Biosphere 2 WALD ¹³CO₂ drought study with shotgun-metagenomic and 16S-amplicon profiling of the soil microbiome — adding a taxonomic "who is present" axis to the ¹³C carbon-flux measurements in the manuscript. Shotgun profiling with sylph (GTDB r232 + FungiRefSeq + custom AMF database); 36 samples passing QC across pre-drought and drought conditions.

Headline: At n=36, no taxon survives multiple-testing correction and overall diversity/structure is unchanged — no statistically significant drought shift. The strongest pattern, by converging evidence rather than any single p-value, is suppression of nitrite-oxidising Nitrospira (large effect, consistent phylum→genus→strain, echoed in the 328-sample 16S survey), with a drought-tolerant Actinomycetota rise. We report these as hypotheses, not established effects. Key caveat: pre-drought samples are from September and drought from November, so condition is confounded with sampling date/season (§3b).

1. Community composition

The soil is bacteria-dominated (~95%), with a substantial ammonia-oxidising archaeal fraction (Thermoproteota/Nitrososphaeria, ~16%). Proteobacteria (Pseudomonadota) make up about half of all classified reads. Mean composition is near-identical between conditions — the drought signal is in specific taxa, not the bulk.

Why this matters: Actinomycetota are textbook drought-tolerant soil bacteria (thick peptidoglycan walls, sporulation, osmolyte accumulation). Their relative increase is an independent confirmation that the drought treatment imposed real water stress on the soil community. The Nitrospirota decline points to drought-suppressed nitrification — a functionally meaningful change in N-cycling.

2b. Genus level sharpens this into a nitrogen-cycling signal

Resolving to genus pinpoints the functional groups behind the phylum shifts. The strongest single response in the entire dataset is the nitrite-oxidiser Nitrospira, which drops from 0.86% to 0.28% (p=0.0009, q=0.07, Cliff's δ=-0.63 — large effect) — i.e. the Nitrospirota phylum decline is a Nitrospira decline, a direct hit to nitrification. Alongside it the nitrogen-fixer / legume root symbiont Bradyrhizobium falls (0.55→0.31%, p=0.031), while the methylotroph Methyloceanibacter rises (5.3→7.7%, p=0.031).

Coherent N-cycle suppression: drought reduces both nitrite-oxidation (Nitrospira) and nitrogen-fixation (Bradyrhizobium) — two complementary nitrogen-cycle functions moving down together. The Bradyrhizobium signal is notable given the belowground role of the legume C. fairchildiana in the manuscript.
Why phylum leads the report: of 70 testable genera, 44 are uninterpretable GTDB placeholder codes (e.g. DATFRX01), and the Actinomycetota phylum increase does not resolve to any single genus — it is spread thinly across many. Genus level adds functional specificity for the nitrogen-cyclers but is noisier overall, so the robust headline stays at phylum.

2c. Species level: a strain-specific signal in a largely uncultured community

Two things stand out at species/strain resolution. First, this is a predominantly uncultured community: on average 49% of classified abundance (range 25–63%) belongs to GTDB lineages with no cultured representative — placeholder-coded single-cell and metagenome-assembled genomes (e.g. the most abundant single "species", at ~17%, is an uncultured organism, SCGC-AG-212-J23). This fits a long-enclosed artificial ecosystem harbouring novel soil taxa.

Second, the nitrogen-cycle signal sharpens to a single strain: the nitrite-oxidiser decline tracks one dominant Nitrospira genome (GCA_029194675.1), down 0.75→0.28% (p=0.001, q=0.12). Critically, the same decline is present at every taxonomic rank — phylum → genus → strain (raw p<0.05 throughout; q≈0.07–0.12 after correction) — which, together with its large effect size and independent 16S reproduction, makes drought-suppressed nitrification the most internally consistent observation in the dataset. It nonetheless does not survive compositional or plot-level correction (§3b), so we frame it as a strong hypothesis rather than an established effect.

3. Diversity and community structure: no detectable shift

Genus-level Shannon diversity does not differ between conditions (pre 2.82 vs drought 2.67, p=0.27). In Bray–Curtis ordination, drought and pre-drought samples fully intermix, and neither community structure (PERMANOVA p=0.31) nor dispersion (PERMDISP p=0.52) differs significantly.

The composition is also stable across the ¹³C pulse-chase window: within each condition, community structure does not differ between the 0 h, 6 h and 48 h sampling timepoints (PERMANOVA p=0.79 drought, p=0.88 pre-drought; 0 of 11–13 phyla shift). This confirms the standing DNA community does not turn over during labelling — so the ¹³C-activity dynamics in the manuscript reflect metabolic shifts within a stable community, and pooling timepoints for the drought contrast introduces no confound.

Markers encode the design: in the first ordination colour = condition and shape = timepoint; in the second colour = site (Site1/Site2/CTRL) and shape = condition. Sites intermix completely.

Note on "convergence": the manuscript describes drought reducing site-to-site variation in ¹³C-utilisation. At the level of bulk taxonomic composition we do not see a matching convergence — within-condition dissimilarity is comparable (pre 0.35, drought 0.39), and a direct between-site test (Site1 vs Site2 Bray–Curtis, pre vs drought) shows no convergence either (pre 0.37, drought 0.39, one-sided p=0.87; sites diverge slightly, if anything, under drought). The two measurements capture different things: the DNA profile here is who-is-present, while the ¹³C-PLFA signal is who-is-active. A stable DNA pool alongside shifting carbon flux is fully consistent — the active sub-community can converge without the standing community changing.

4. Independent validation: 16S rRNA amplicon survey

A separate 16S rRNA amplicon dataset (V4 515F/806R) from the same Biosphere 2 soil was re-analysed with current tools (QIIME 2 2026.4, DADA2, Greengenes2 + SILVA 138.1): 328 samples, 34,594 ASVs — an order of magnitude more samples than the shotgun set, providing an independent cross-check.

The two methods agree on the community. The amplicon recovers 12 of 13 shotgun-detected phyla and both rank the same groups as dominant (Pseudomonadota, Acidobacteriota, Actinomycetota, Methylomirabilota, Thermoproteota). Abundance magnitudes differ as expected from method bias (16S copy-number vs WGS genome-detection), but the identity of the dominant community is reproducible across two independent assays.

Cross-method drought response. Restricting the amplicon data to its own drought-labelled subset (15 pre vs 20 drought) and testing the two headline phyla, both move in the same direction as the shotgun data:

The shifts are statistically significant in the shotgun data and directionally concordant — though not significant — in the smaller, non-paired amplicon subset. The drought-tolerant Actinomycetota increase and the Nitrospirota (nitrifier) decline are thus the most robust signals, reproducing across two assays and two sample sets.

Honest caveats: the amplicon drought subset is small (n=15/20) and is not the same physical samples as the shotgun set, so cross-method agreement is at the level of biological signal, not paired replicates. The amplicon also flags its own shifts (Planctomycetota ▲ p=0.02, Bacteroidota ▼ p=0.03) not seen in the WGS — phylum-level drought responses beyond Actinomycetota/Nitrospirota are method-dependent and should be treated as provisional. 16S primers are prokaryote-only (no fungi); notably the one "fungal" hit is Glomeribacter, the bacterial endosymbiont that lives inside AMF spores — a trace of the mycorrhizal fungi the 16S cannot otherwise see.

5. Eukaryotes and mycorrhizal fungi

SSU-based screening (phyloFlash/SILVA) detects eukaryotic and plant signal in every sample, confirming root/fungal material in the soil. However, SILVA cannot resolve arbuscular (AMF) or ectomycorrhizal (EMF) fungi below the Opisthokonta level, and sylph's genome-containment approach finds no AMF/EMF (they are too low-coverage in bulk shotgun data). Resolving the AMF/EMF community — the mycorrhizal symbionts central to the belowground carbon story in the manuscript — requires read-level protein classification against a custom AMF/EMF-enriched database (kaiju nr_euk + AMF + EMF, in progress).

Methods (brief) — full detailed methods →

Reads: 39 paired-end Illumina soil metagenomes (Biosphere 2 WALD campaign, 2019). Profiling: sylph against GTDB r232, FungiRefSeq-2025, and a dereplicated 32-genome AMF database; taxonomic tables via sylph-tax. Independent 16S: V4 515F/806R EMP amplicons, QIIME 2 2026.4 + DADA2 1.38 + Greengenes2 2024.09 / SILVA 138.1 (328 samples, 34,594 ASVs); phylum (L2) relative abundances compared to the WGS profile. Statistics on genus/phylum relative abundances: Mann–Whitney U (taxa) with Benjamini–Hochberg FDR (q-values) and Cliff's δ effect sizes; PERMANOVA & PERMDISP on Bray–Curtis distances (999 permutations). 36/39 shotgun samples passed sketch QC — the 3 dropped (2 CTRL, 1 Site1) leave the CTRL site with a single shotgun sample, so cross-site contrasts rest on Site1 vs Site2. Timepoints (0/6/48 h) are repeated measures of the same plots; p-values treat samples as independent and are thus anticonservative, so taxon claims are anchored on effect size and cross-assay reproduction rather than p alone.
