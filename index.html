Soil microbiome response to drought in the Biosphere 2 tropical rainforest

Shotgun-metagenomic and 16S-amplicon profiling of the soil microbiome from the Biosphere 2 WALD ¹³CO₂ drought experiment — the standing-community counterpart to the ¹³C–PLFA carbon-flux study (Bai et al.). 39 metagenomes across pre-drought and drought; taxonomic profiling with sylph, Kaiju and DIAMOND, functional profiling with NCyc and dbCAN. Detailed methods →

Summary. The standing soil community is compositionally stable across the drought: no taxon survives multiple-testing correction and α- and β-diversity are unchanged. The most consistent shift is a decline in the nitrite-oxidiser Nitrospira, reproduced from phylum to strain and across four independent methods (sylph, Kaiju, DIAMOND, 16S), with a parallel decline in the N-fixer Bradyrhizobium and an increase in Actinomycetota. This signal does not reach significance once pseudoreplication is accounted for, and condition is confounded with season (pre-drought = September, drought = November). Functional gene potential (N-cycling, CAZymes) is likewise unchanged. Together this indicates the drought response is a re-routing of carbon allocation to fungal partners (companion ¹³C study) rather than a turnover of the standing community or its gene repertoire.

1. Community composition

The soil is bacteria-dominated (~95%), with a substantial ammonia-oxidising archaeal fraction (Thermoproteota/Nitrososphaeria, ~16%). Proteobacteria (Pseudomonadota) make up about half of all classified reads. Mean composition is near-identical between conditions — the drought signal is in specific taxa, not the bulk.

Actinomycetota are characteristically drought-tolerant (thick peptidoglycan walls, sporulation, osmolyte accumulation), so their relative increase indicates the treatment imposed real water stress on the community; the Nitrospirota decline points to suppressed nitrification.

2b. Genus level sharpens this into a nitrogen-cycling signal

Resolving to genus pinpoints the functional groups behind the phylum shifts. The strongest single response in the entire dataset is the nitrite-oxidiser Nitrospira, which drops from 0.81% to 0.25% (p=0.0011, q=0.11, Cliff's δ=-0.59 — large effect) — i.e. the Nitrospirota phylum decline is a Nitrospira decline, a direct hit to nitrification. Alongside it the nitrogen-fixer / legume root symbiont Bradyrhizobium falls (0.52→0.28%, p=0.027), while the methylotroph Methyloceanibacter rises (5.3→7.7%, p=0.019).

Both nitrite-oxidation (Nitrospira) and nitrogen-fixation (Bradyrhizobium) decline together — a coherent suppression of two complementary N-cycle functions. The Bradyrhizobium decline is notable given the belowground role of the legume C. fairchildiana in the companion ¹³C study.
Why phylum leads the report: of 70 testable genera, 44 are uninterpretable GTDB placeholder codes (e.g. DATFRX01), and the Actinomycetota phylum increase does not resolve to any single genus — it is spread thinly across many. Genus level adds functional specificity for the nitrogen-cyclers but is noisier overall, so the robust headline stays at phylum.

2c. Species level: a strain-specific signal in a largely uncultured community

Two things stand out at species/strain resolution. First, this is a predominantly uncultured community: on average 79% of classified abundance (range 58–88%) belongs to GTDB lineages with no cultured representative — placeholder-coded single-cell and metagenome-assembled genomes (e.g. the most abundant single "species", at ~17%, is an uncultured organism, SCGC-AG-212-J23). This fits a long-enclosed artificial ecosystem harbouring novel soil taxa.

Second, the nitrogen-cycle signal sharpens to a single strain: the nitrite-oxidiser decline tracks one dominant Nitrospira genome (GCA_029194675.1), down 0.71→0.25% (p=0.001, q=0.22). Critically, the same decline is present at every taxonomic rank — phylum → genus → strain (raw p<0.05 throughout; q≈0.07–0.12 after correction) — which, together with its large effect size and independent 16S reproduction, makes drought-suppressed nitrification the most internally consistent observation in the dataset. It nonetheless does not survive compositional or plot-level correction (§3b), so we frame it as a strong hypothesis rather than an established effect.

3. Diversity and community structure: no detectable shift

Genus-level Shannon diversity does not differ between conditions (pre 2.78 vs drought 2.62, p=0.22). In Bray–Curtis ordination, drought and pre-drought samples fully intermix, and neither community structure (PERMANOVA p=0.15) nor dispersion (PERMDISP p=0.50) differs significantly.

The composition is also stable across the ¹³C pulse-chase window: within each condition, community structure does not differ between the 0 h, 6 h and 48 h sampling timepoints (PERMANOVA p=0.79 drought, p=0.88 pre-drought; 0 of 11–13 phyla shift). This confirms the standing DNA community does not turn over during labelling — so the ¹³C-activity dynamics in the manuscript reflect metabolic shifts within a stable community, and pooling timepoints for the drought contrast introduces no confound.

Markers encode the design: in the first ordination colour = condition and shape = timepoint; in the second colour = site (Site1/Site2/CTRL) and shape = condition. Sites intermix completely.

Note on "convergence": the manuscript describes drought reducing site-to-site variation in ¹³C-utilisation. At the level of bulk taxonomic composition we do not see a matching convergence — within-condition dissimilarity is comparable (pre 0.36, drought 0.38), and a direct between-site test (Site1 vs Site2 Bray–Curtis, pre vs drought) shows no convergence either (pre 0.37, drought 0.39, one-sided p=0.84; sites diverge slightly, if anything, under drought). The two measurements capture different things: the DNA profile here is who-is-present, while the ¹³C-PLFA signal is who-is-active. A stable DNA pool alongside shifting carbon flux is fully consistent — the active sub-community can converge without the standing community changing.

4. Independent validation: 16S rRNA amplicon survey

A separate 16S rRNA amplicon dataset (V4 515F/806R) from the same Biosphere 2 soil was re-analysed with current tools (QIIME 2 2026.4, DADA2, Greengenes2 + SILVA 138.1): 328 samples, 34,594 ASVs — an order of magnitude more samples than the shotgun set, providing an independent cross-check.

The two methods agree on the community. The amplicon recovers 12 of 13 shotgun-detected phyla and both rank the same groups as dominant (Pseudomonadota, Acidobacteriota, Actinomycetota, Methylomirabilota, Thermoproteota). Abundance magnitudes differ as expected from method bias (16S copy-number vs WGS genome-detection), but the identity of the dominant community is reproducible across two independent assays.

Cross-method drought response. Restricting the amplicon data to its own drought-labelled subset (15 pre vs 20 drought) and testing the two headline phyla, both move in the same direction as the shotgun data:

The shifts are statistically significant in the shotgun data and directionally concordant — though not significant — in the smaller, non-paired amplicon subset. The drought-tolerant Actinomycetota increase and the Nitrospirota (nitrifier) decline are thus the most robust signals, reproducing across two assays and two sample sets.

Honest caveats: the amplicon drought subset is small (n=15/20) and is not the same physical samples as the shotgun set, so cross-method agreement is at the level of biological signal, not paired replicates. The amplicon also flags its own shifts (Planctomycetota ▲ p=0.02, Bacteroidota ▼ p=0.03) not seen in the WGS — phylum-level drought responses beyond Actinomycetota/Nitrospirota are method-dependent and should be treated as provisional. 16S primers are prokaryote-only (no fungi); notably the one "fungal" hit is Glomeribacter, the bacterial endosymbiont that lives inside AMF spores — a trace of the mycorrhizal fungi the 16S cannot otherwise see.

4b. Protein-level cross-validation (Kaiju + DIAMOND)

Two protein aligners classified the reads against the same nr_euk database. They agree closely (median per-sample Spearman ρ=0.87; 10/15 top genera shared) and identify the same dominant taxa — the nitrogen-cyclers and rhizosphere bacteria Bradyrhizobium, Nitrospira, Luteitalea and Mesorhizobium. Both record the drought decline in Nitrospira (DIAMOND 5.6→4.7%, Kaiju 3.5→3.0% of classified reads). The decline therefore holds across all four methods applied here — sylph, Kaiju, DIAMOND and 16S — the most reproducible result in the dataset, though still short of significance after correction for pseudoreplication (§3b).

5. Eukaryotes and mycorrhizal fungi

SSU screening (phyloFlash/SILVA) detects eukaryotic and plant signal in every sample (confirming root/fungal material), but cannot resolve arbuscular (AMF) or ectomycorrhizal (EMF) fungi below Opisthokonta, and sylph genome-containment finds no AMF/EMF (too low-coverage in bulk shotgun).

Resolving the mycorrhizal community needs read-level protein classification against a fungal-enriched database. All 39 samples were classified with a custom Kaiju fungal DB (nr_euk fungi + 32 AMF genomes + EMF references), recovering the AMF/EMF genera that SSU surveys and genome-containment miss.

Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF) are present, dominated by Rhizophagus (≈4.7% of fungal reads; fungi are 3.2% of all reads). Further AMF: Oehlia, Acaulospora, Paraglomus, Diversispora. Ectomycorrhizal signal is lower (≈1.6% of fungal reads), led by Russula and Tuber. Full AMF/EMF genus matrices (% of fungal and of total reads) + drought tests: companion Excel (GBI_AMF_EMF_kaiju.xlsx).
AMF/EMF reads are a small fraction of bulk-soil shotgun data, so genus proportions carry wide per-sample variance — read as presence / relative dominance, not precise quantities. No AMF/EMF genus shows a drought shift surviving FDR.

5b. Functional potential: nitrogen-cycle genes

Shotgun reads were profiled against NCyc (68 nitrogen-cycle gene families; DIAMOND). The community's N-cycling gene pool is dominated by denitrification (≈750 hits per million reads) and DNRA (≈475), with a smaller dedicated nitrification set.

The nitrite-oxidation gene pool is buffered even though Nitrospira declines. Drought leaves nxrAB (nitrite oxidoreductase) essentially unchanged (16.5→16.4 hits/million reads) and ammonia-oxidation amoABC only marginally lower (7.3→6.6); no N-cycle gene family survives FDR (1 significant). So the strong taxonomic decline of Nitrospira (§2b, §4b) is not mirrored by a loss of nitrite-oxidation gene potential — consistent with functional redundancy (other taxa also carry nxr) and with the abundance≠function theme (§6): the standing community's N-cycling capacity is buffered against this drought.

Carbohydrate-active enzymes (CAZymes; dbCAN, 4.1 M sequences). The same pattern holds on the carbon side: glycoside-hydrolase (GH; carbohydrate/litter degradation) and auxiliary-activity (AA; ligninolytic/redox) gene potential are unchanged under drought (GH 20702→20483; AA 1547→1519 hits/million reads) and no CAZy class or family survives FDR (0 significant). The community's degradative gene repertoire is buffered too — so the ¹³C drought response is a re-routing of carbon to fungal partners (§6), not a change in the standing pool of carbohydrate-degrading or nitrogen-cycling genes.

Osmolyte / compatible-solute genes. Because the companion study's central plant response is investment in osmolytes, we asked whether soil microbes show the analogous signal — enrichment of compatible-solute biosynthesis genes (trehalose, ectoine, glycine-betaine, glucosylglycerol, mannitol; curated UniProt set). They do not: total osmotic-stress gene potential is unchanged (1556→1553 hits/million reads, p=0.70; 0/6 pathways significant). The microbial drought response, if any, is therefore not encoded in a shift of genomic osmolyte potential — pointing to regulation at the transcript/metabolite level (which DNA cannot resolve), unlike the plants' standing osmolyte investment.

Synthesis — one axis moves, the rest are buffered. Across taxonomy, N-cycle, carbohydrate-active and osmolyte genes, only the taxonomic decline of nitrite-oxidisers reaches a non-trivial effect size; functional gene potential is flat.

6. Discussion: the metagenome in the context of the ¹³C–PLFA companion study

This shotgun survey is the standing-community counterpart to the B2 WALD ¹³C–PLFA companion study (Bai, Shi, Huang … Werner, Dippold)[1], which traced recent photosynthate through the rhizosphere of the same drought campaign[2]. Under drought that study found trees invested fresh carbon mainly into topsoil fine-root water-soluble carbon (osmolytes) — not deeper roots or rhizodeposition — and re-routed recent C to fungi in a compartment-specific way: AMF gained ¹³C in the still-moist subsoil (especially the legume Clitoria fairchildiana), while saprotrophic + ectomycorrhizal fungi gained ¹³C in the dried topsoil (the single largest allocation shift). Which bacteria received C was set by root metabolic / exudate control, not by Gram+/Gram− desiccation tolerance.

Why our flat metagenome is concordant, not contradictory. The companion study's own PLFA community composition was largely buffered: only the dried topsoil rhizosphere shifted significantly, subsoil communities did not, and AMF biomarkers were present under both ambient and drought conditions. Its dramatic drought signal lived in ¹³C allocation — who is actively taking up new carbon — not in standing biomass. Our metagenome measures exactly that buffered axis (standing DNA) and agrees with it: no taxon survives correction, diversity and structure are unchanged, and all fungal guilds are flat (total fungi 3.2→3.1% of reads). Read-abundance is simply blind to the isotope-allocation axis where the companion signal sits.
  • Abundance ≠ activity — both methods show it. ¹³C–PLFA tracks carbon flux into active lipid synthesis (hours–days); shotgun reads and PLFA mol% both track standing community structure. The drought response here is an allocation/flux response that only isotope tracing resolves — neither abundance method should be expected to mirror it.
  • Actinomycetota — a concrete case. Our WGS shows a drought rise in Actinomycetota relative abundance, yet the companion ¹³C data show Actinobacteria are not preferentially fed fresh carbon under drought (lowest enrichment; constant organic-matter degraders with desiccation-resistant spores)[1]. The abundance rise reflects drought persistence, not active uptake of recent plant C — abundance and activity pointing in different directions within the same system.
  • AMF/fungi flat ≈ "present under both." Our flat AMF/EMF read-abundance matches the PLFA picture (AMF biomarkers in both conditions); the AMF response is a subsoil ¹³C-allocation effect, below the detection floor of bulk-soil shotgun (AMF are ~6% of an already ~3% fungal fraction).
  • Bacteria — function over taxonomy. The companion work attributes carbon flow to root exudate / metabolic control rather than cell-wall tolerance, with functional capacity retained even as taxa reshuffle[1,3] — consistent with our result that no clean taxonomic abundance signal survives correction; the drought response is metabolic, not compositional. (Our declining nitrifier Nitrospira is an N-cycle read-out the C-focused PLFA study does not target, but is consistent with drought-suppressed nitrification.)
  • Compartment & resolution. The companion study is rhizosphere, depth- and tree-species-resolved; our shotgun set is neither, and carries a Sept-vs-Nov seasonal offset (§3b) — so it cannot resolve the topsoil-vs-subsoil or species-specific effects (e.g. the C. fairchildiana subsoil-AMF flux) that carry the signal.

Both findings are consistent with the wider literature. The Nitrospira decline matches Séneca et al.[5], who found comammox Nitrospira amoA (gene and transcript) dropped significantly under experimental soil drought while bulk nitrification rate was maintained by ammonia-oxidising bacteria — the same nitrite-oxidiser sensitivity and functional compensation we see (taxonomic decline, buffered nxr potential). More broadly, the decoupling of a perturbation-sensitive taxonomy from a stable functional gene pool is the expected signature of functional redundancy in soil, repeatedly reported in global soil metagenomics[6] and reviews of microbial resistance/resilience to climate extremes[7]. Our result therefore places the B2 rainforest soil within the common "compositionally responsive, functionally buffered" regime.

Bottom line: the metagenome and the ¹³C–PLFA study are concordant where they overlap (a compositionally buffered standing community) and complementary where they do not (¹³C carbon allocation). “No abundance shift” does not refute “more carbon to fungi” — they are different axes. The definitive bridge is DNA- / quantitative-SIP metagenomics with depth and compartment resolution (heavy-fraction sequencing of the ¹³C-labelled active community)[4] — the natural follow-up this survey cannot itself provide.

Statistical considerations

Four points underlie reporting the Nitrospira shift as a hypothesis rather than an established effect. Pseudoreplication: the 0/6/48 h timepoints are repeated measures of the same plots, not independent samples; a plot random-effect mixed model and a plot-collapsed test both remove the nominal significance (§3b). Multiple testing: with Benjamini–Hochberg FDR across taxa, no taxon reaches q<0.05. Effect size: nonetheless Nitrospira's Cliff's δ ≈ -0.59 is large (drought samples are almost always lower), so the direction is consistent even where it is not significant. Power: at n = 18 vs 21 only very large effects (Cohen's d ≈ 0.924) are detectable, so a non-significant result here means "not detectable at this sample size", not "no effect". The signal is therefore anchored on effect size and cross-method reproduction rather than p-values.

Methods (brief) — full detailed methods →

Reads: 39 paired-end Illumina soil metagenomes (Biosphere 2 WALD campaign, 2019). Profiling: sylph against GTDB r232, FungiRefSeq-2025, and a dereplicated 32-genome AMF database; taxonomic tables via sylph-tax. Independent 16S: V4 515F/806R EMP amplicons, QIIME 2 2026.4 + DADA2 1.38 + Greengenes2 2024.09 / SILVA 138.1 (328 samples, 34,594 ASVs); phylum (L2) relative abundances compared to the WGS profile. Protein-level classification of all 39 metagenomes against a cleaned nr_euk database with Kaiju (MEM) and DIAMOND 2.2.1 (double-indexed, -f102 LCA; 5M-read subsample per sample concatenated into one search), plus a custom Kaiju fungal DB (nr_euk-fungi + 32 AMF genomes + EMF references) for the AMF/EMF breakdown; the nr_euk database build required stripping internal * separators from the source FASTA. Statistics on genus/phylum relative abundances: Mann–Whitney U (taxa) with Benjamini–Hochberg FDR (q-values) and Cliff's δ effect sizes; PERMANOVA & PERMDISP on Bray–Curtis distances (999 permutations). 39/39 shotgun samples processed — 3 (2 CTRL, 1 Site1) initially failed sylph sketching because of corrupted reads from a faulty parallel download (a few records with malformed quality strings) and were recovered with seqkit sana + re-pairing before re-profiling. Timepoints (0/6/48 h) are repeated measures of the same plots; p-values treat samples as independent and are thus anticonservative, so taxon claims are anchored on effect size and cross-assay reproduction rather than p alone. Figures and tables reproducible from /mnt/disk4/timo/gbi/b2/analysis/ and /mnt/disk4/timo/gbi/b2/amplicon/reanalysis_2026/.
